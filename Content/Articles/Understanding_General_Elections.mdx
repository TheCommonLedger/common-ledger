## What Is a General Election?

A **general election** is the election that determines **who actually holds public office**.

While primaries decide *who runs*, the general election decides **who wins**.

In the United States, general elections are held on the **first Tuesday after the first Monday in November** during even-numbered years.

This is the point where campaigns end — and governing begins.

---

## Where General Elections Fit in the Process

Think of elections as a sequence:

**Primary Elections → General Elections → Governing**

- **Primary elections** select party nominees  
- **General elections** select officeholders  
- **Winners** assume constitutional authority  

Each stage shapes the next. Skipping understanding at any step weakens participation at the end.

---

## What Appears on a General Election Ballot?

Depending on the year and your location, general election ballots may include:

### Federal Offices
- President & Vice President (every 4 years)
- U.S. House of Representatives (all 435 seats, every 2 years)
- U.S. Senate (about one-third of seats, every 2 years)

### State Offices
- Governors
- State legislators
- Attorneys general, secretaries of state, treasurers (varies by state)

### Local Offices
- Mayors
- City councils
- County officials
- Judges (in many states)

### Ballot Measures
- Constitutional amendments
- Referendums and initiatives
- Bond and tax proposals

General elections determine **policy direction at every level**.

---

## Who Can Vote in a General Election?

Unlike primaries, **general elections are open to all registered voters**, regardless of party affiliation.

If you are:
- A registered Democrat
- A registered Republican
- An independent or unaffiliated voter

You may vote in the general election.

This is where the **broadest public voice** is expressed.

---

## How Voting Works

Most states offer several voting options:

- In-person voting on Election Day  
- Early in-person voting  
- Absentee or mail-in voting (rules vary by state)

After votes are cast:
1. Ballots are counted  
2. Results are canvassed  
3. Votes are certified  
4. Winners take office  

Certification — not media projections — determines outcomes.

---

## Why General Elections Matter

General elections decide:

- Who writes laws
- Who controls budgets
- Who oversees agencies
- Who appoints judges
- Who represents communities

Low turnout doesn’t preserve neutrality — it **concentrates power**.

Participation is how consent of the governed is expressed in practice.

## Common Misconceptions

❌ *“My vote doesn’t matter.”*  
✅ Many elections are decided by small margins.

❌ *“Only presidential races matter.”*  
✅ Congressional, state, and local offices often affect daily life more directly.

❌ *“The outcome is already set.”*  
✅ Turnout regularly changes results.

---

## The Constitutional Foundation

General elections are grounded in the U.S. Constitution:

- **Article I** — Congress  
- **Article II** — President  
- **Article VI** — Oaths and lawful authority  

They are not symbolic exercises. They are **constitutional mechanisms for self-government**.
