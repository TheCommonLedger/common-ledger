**Dear Readers,**

I want to take a moment to step back from the headlines and speak plainly.

America is under strain—but America is not broken. And it is not accurately represented by the most alarming narratives dominating our screens.

What we are seeing across the country, including in places like Minnesota, is not the collapse of civil society. It is the collision of fear, misinformation, and speed—where incomplete or misleading stories travel faster than facts, and emotion is rewarded more than understanding.

That dynamic should concern all of us, regardless of politics.

Disagreement is not the danger. Protest is not the danger. Even anger, in itself, is not the danger. The danger lies in how quickly well-intentioned people can be drawn into reacting before verifying, defending before understanding, and choosing sides before truth has had time to surface.

Too often, good people come to believe they are protecting victims or standing against injustice, only to later discover they were acting on narratives that were oversimplified, distorted, or false. The consequences can be devastating—for individuals, for communities, and for trust itself.

This does not happen because Americans are ignorant or malicious. It happens because Americans care. And in an environment driven by clicks, outrage, and constant urgency, care can be exploited.

There are actors—political, ideological, and financial—who benefit from division and instability. They do not bear the cost of fractured communities or damaged lives. They profit from escalation, from confusion, and from the erosion of confidence in shared facts. When everything feels like an emergency, discernment becomes difficult—and manipulation becomes easier.

In moments like this, suspicion becomes widespread. Institutions are questioned. Motives are doubted. Claims of corruption, fraud, and bad faith circulate rapidly, often faster than evidence can be responsibly examined. Some of these concerns may prove valid. Others will not. But when trust collapses entirely, the damage is the same: people retreat into camps, assume the worst of one another, and stop listening.

That outcome serves no ordinary American.

Patriotism, at its best, is not loyalty to a party, a figure, or a narrative. It is loyalty to principles: fairness, accountability, human dignity, and truth. It is the discipline to question without dehumanizing, to challenge power without discarding restraint, and to care deeply without surrendering judgment.

I do not hold public office. I do not claim special authority. But as editor of *The Common Ledger*, I do carry a responsibility—to slow things down, to resist frenzy, and to insist on careful examination when others demand instant conclusions.

This publication exists to separate signal from noise, to approach claims with skepticism and humility, and to remember that clarity is more valuable than certainty. Our aim is not to inflame, but to inform; not to divide, but to ground.

If there is one message I hope readers take from this letter, it is this: **you are not powerless, and you are not obligated to react to every provocation placed before you.** You can pause. You can verify. You can refuse to let fear or outrage dictate your view of your fellow citizens.

America has faced moments of intense division before. What carried us through was not uniformity of opinion, but a shared commitment to truth, restraint, and the belief that our neighbors are more than the worst story told about them.

Those values still matter. And they are still enough.

At *The Common Ledger*, we will continue to do our work with care, independence, and respect for our readers—trusting that a steady hand is more useful than a clenched fist in times like these.

Thank you for reading.  
Thank you for thinking.  
And thank you for choosing discernment in an age that profits from its absence.

**— Cory Capps**  
*Editor, The Common Ledger*
